Introduction: Exploring Australia's Economic Reliance on Iron Ore

This dashboard explores how deeply Australia's economy is connected to iron ore through production scale, trade concentration, and logistics pathways.

The key theme is whether Australia's mining strength reflects durable resilience or a dependence on a narrow set of buyers and maritime routes. We identify where Australia's advantage is strongest, and where concentration risk is highest.

Key Insight: The Fragile Trade Pathway

Australia's iron ore trade is highly concentrated, with most ore mined in Western Australia, exported through a small number of ports, and shipped along the same maritime routes to China. This creates an extremely efficient but fragile system, where disruptions to shared shipping corridors, regional infrastructure, or Chinese demand can impact the entire Australian export network at once.

Part 3: Price Shock Risk

Iron Ore Price vs. Australian GDP Growth — A Coupled Trajectory

Each point is a year (2010–2024), connected chronologically. The path traces how Australia's economic growth rate tracks the iron ore price cycle — revealing the structural coupling between commodity prices and national prosperity.

Figure 9
Key Findings:

The trajectory reveals two distinct regimes: the 2010–2014 boom — high prices, stable growth — and the 2015–2016 bust — prices halved from $168 to $56/t while growth decelerated. The 2021 price spike to $162/t briefly rescued growth after COVID, but the subsequent slide toward ~$108/t by 2024 is compressing GDP growth back to 1.2%. The path is never random — it loops with the iron ore cycle.

A sustained fall below $80/t — plausible under Simandou expansion — would push Australia into growth territory not seen since the GFC.

Section 2 Takeaway: A Volatile Engine for a Whole Economy

Iron ore is not one commodity among many — it makes up 20% of total exports and 40% of mineral exports, making it the engine of the Australian economy. That engine runs hot and cold: export revenue nearly tripled from AUD 74B in 2014 to AUD 184B in 2022, then fell 30% by 2024, all within a single decade. The royalty flow is also heavily privatised — of AUD 131B in exports, AUD 42B flows to shareholder profits before public revenue channels see significant returns. China's steel demand and Australian exports move in structural lockstep, meaning any slowdown in Chinese construction directly cuts Australian income. Meanwhile, Simandou looms as a supply shock: even under stable demand, Guinea's new iron ore project could depress prices by 20–30%, compressing Australian margins without any change in volumes. Australia earns enormously from iron ore, but has limited control over the price, the buyer, or the emerging competition.

Section 3 Takeaway: The Case for Managed Diversification

The radar chart makes Australia's vulnerability structural and comparative — not just bad luck. Peer nations like Norway built sovereign wealth funds during their resource booms; Australia did not match that pace. Canada developed a parallel manufacturing export base; Australia's remains thin. These are policy choices, not geography, and they can be reversed.

The opportunity ladder shows the path forward is real. Seven alternative sectors collectively hold the capacity to grow from roughly AUD 113B to AUD 285B by 2035. Green hydrogen alone could become a $50B export industry — larger than iron ore was in 2014 — if Australia captures its natural advantage in renewable energy generation. Critical minerals offer a similar compounding opportunity as the global green transition accelerates demand for lithium, cobalt, and nickel.

The portfolio scenario chart frames the stakes as a choice, not a forecast. Business-as-usual leaves Australia almost as exposed in 2035 as today — iron ore and coal still accounting for over 40% of exports. Under managed diversification, that drops to under 25%, giving the economy far more buffer against price shocks, Chinese demand slowdowns, or the Simandou supply surge modelled earlier.

The window is narrowing. Global supply chains for green minerals and hydrogen are being established now. First-mover advantage in these markets will define the next 30 years of Australian trade in the same way the China iron ore boom defined the last 20. The risk of over-concentration is known; the tools to address it exist; the question is whether the policy will follow the evidence.

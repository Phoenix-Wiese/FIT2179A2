Introduction: Exploring Australia's Economic Reliance on Iron Ore

This dashboard explores how deeply Australia's economy is connected to iron ore through production scale, trade concentration, and logistics pathways. The key theme is whether Australia's mining strength reflects durable resilience or a dependence on a narrow set of buyers and maritime routes. Focus: identify where Australia's advantage is strongest, and where concentration risk is highest.

Full Section Takeaway

Australia’s iron ore trade is highly concentrated, with most ore mined in Western Australia, exported through a small number of ports, and shipped along the same maritime routes to China. This creates an extremely efficient but fragile system, where disruptions to shared shipping corridors, regional infrastructure, or Chinese demand can impact the entire Australian export network at once. The key issue is that production, transport, and demand are all dependent on the same narrow trade pathway, creating significant economic and geopolitical instability.

Australia Export Composition Over Time

Annual export values by category (AUD billions), 2014-2024.

Analysis: Iron ore rises from AUD 74B (2014) to a peak of AUD 184B (2022), then falls to AUD 131B (2024). Other Exports grow from AUD 145B to AUD 231B over the same period (up AUD 86B). Iron is one of Australia's most important exports, making up 20% of total exports, 5% of Australia's GDP and 40% of mineral exports alone. Key takeaway: iron ore drives volatility, but broader export categories have grown strongly.

Key takeaway: the largest visible allocations are corporate returns and federal channels, with smaller but direct public-service flows.

Simandou Risk Scenarios

Chinese demand has been strongly correlated with Australian exports, showing how much of Australia's mining sector and China's steel production is highly coupled. This lack of diversity repersents a major threat to both countries, as any shock to one side or rising tensions could cause a rapid feedback loop of economic harm. Chinese officials have expressed concern about this dependence, and are actively seeking to diversify supply through new projects like Simandou in Guinea. The Simandou risk scenarios show that even under stable demand, prices could fall sharply if new supply comes online. A major slowdown in Chinese property development would also cause a significant price drop, while the green steel transition could create long-term downward pressure on demand and prices, Harming australian export revenues even if Chinese demand remains stable.
